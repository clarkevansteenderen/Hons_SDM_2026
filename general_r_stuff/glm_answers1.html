Practice exercise 1

The data set pollinator_abundance_data.csv contains pollinator abundances on three different flower species, across three seasons (summer, autumn, and winter).

Run the appropriate GLM on this data, and have a look at whether:

  1. abundance differs across the three flower hosts
  2. abundance differs across seasons
  3. season affects abundance differently across the three flower species

Additionally, answer the following:

  1. What is the response variable?
  2. What is the predictor variable?
  3. Do we need to include an interaction term in this model?
  4. Which GLM family was appropriate for this data? Provide the relevant model diagnostic plots.
  5. Provide box-and-whisker plots to graphically present the data

Answers

  1. The response variable is pollinator_abundance. This is the variable we are focusing on.
  2. The predictor variables are flower_sp and season. We want to see how these predictors change our response variable.
  3. Yes, as we want to see whether season affects pollinator_abundance differently on different flower_sp. If we do not include an interaction term, our model will assume that pollinator_abundance changes with the same magnitude across the different flower_sp over different seasons.
  4. Since we are dealing with count data, the best approaches are the poisson or negative binomial GLM families, depending on the degree of overdispersion.

The boxplots look better than the Gaussian model, but the QQ plot shows that the points are not falling along the y = x line, that the KS test is significant, that there is significant overdispersion and outliers.

Let’s fit a negative binomial model, to account for overdispersion:

# fit a model with just one predictor: flower species
pollinator.glm.negbin.1 = glmmTMB::glmmTMB(pollinator_abundance ~ flower_sp,
                          family = nbinom2(link = "log"),
                          data = pollinator.data)

# fit another model with both flower species and season
pollinator.glm.negbin.2 = glmmTMB::glmmTMB(pollinator_abundance ~ flower_sp + season,
                          family = nbinom2(link = "log"),
                          data = pollinator.data)

# check which model is better -> the model with both predictors is best
anova(pollinator.glm.negbin.1, pollinator.glm.negbin.2, test = "Chisq")

We can thus report that:

There was a significant difference in pollinator abundance recorded on the three flower species (LRT: \(\chi^2\) = 440.97, d.f. = 2, p < 0.001), and a significant difference across seasons (LRT: \(\chi^2\) = 179.53, d.f. = 2, p < 0.001). Additionally, seasonality had a significant effect on the abundance of pollinators across the three different flower species (LRT: \(\chi^2\) = 235.54, d.f. = 4, p < 0.001).

If we have a look at a boxplot, combined with our summary table, we can also add that:

  • Pollinator abundance was highest on flower sp. 3 (28.4 \(\pm\) 19.7), and lowest on flower sp. 1 (10.1 \(\pm\) 6.8)
  • Pollinator abundance was highest in summer (28.5 \(\pm\) 20.9), and lowest in winter (9.1 \(\pm\) 5.9), such that winter abundance was 68% lower: (9.1 - 28.5)/(28.5) x 100
  • For flower sp. 1, pollinator abundance was highest in autumn, while abundances were highest in summer for flower sp. 2 and sp. 3
