Read in the data collected in the 1970s on the smoking habits of 654 youths. Female = 0, male = 1, non-smoker = 0, smoker = 1, FEV = Forced Expiratory Volume (lung capacity). It seems weird that smokers appear to have a higher lung capacity than non-smokers! Split the plots so that they account for age and height:

Let’s fit a few models:

lung.glm.gaus.1 = glm(FEV ~ Smoke, data = lungcap, family = gaussian)
lung.glm.gaus.2 = glm(FEV ~ Smoke + Gender, data = lungcap, family = gaussian)
lung.glm.gaus.3 = glm(FEV ~ Smoke + Gender + Smoke:Gender, 
                      data = lungcap, family = gaussian)

# model 2 is better than 1 -> including gender is NB
anova(lung.glm.gaus.1, lung.glm.gaus.2, test = "Chisq")

The summary output suggests that:

  • The intercept (FEV) is 0.67 when all predictors are zero (age = 0, non-smoker, and female)

  • Smoking (smoker = 1, non-smoker = 0) increases FEV by 2.41 units, compared to a non-smoker (holding all other variables constant)… weird! But, we haven’t accounted for age, gender, or height yet, which comes in with the interaction terms

  • Gender affects FEV, where FEV is 0.73 units less in males than in females (holding all other variables constant)

  • Age affects FEV, where a one year increase in age increases FEV by 0.18 units (holding all other variables constant)

Interactions:

  • The effect of smoking on FEV differs between males and females, where FEV is 0.59 units less in male compared to female smokers. This isn’t significant, however.

  • The effect of smoking on FEV differs across ages, where FEV decreases by 0.19 units with every year increase in age [in smokers], compared to non-smokers

  • The effect of gender on FEV differs across ages, where FEV increases by 0.11 units more in males than females with every year increase in age

  • There is a three-way interaction, suggesting that the effect of smoking on FEV differs between genders, and varies between ages, but this is not significant

Note the sample sizes in this data -> how might this affect our interpretation? The data points for non-smokers is much higher.

We also don’t know what the baseline lung capacity was for participants - there might have been some people with a genetic predisposition for a larger capacity, or maybe some people were physically a lot larger than others. These variables have not been accounted for. Aside from smoking or not, we don’t know what the unit of measurement was: some people might smoke two packets a day, while others smoke only one or two cigarettes per day. There might also be differences in other underlying health issues that could skew the results; perhaps some people have asthma or other lung-related illnesses, which also is not accounted for.
